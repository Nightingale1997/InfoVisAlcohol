Role models or Nanny States?

The nordic countries may have a solution

In order to lower and control alcohol consumption, various goverments have historically tried to ban, restrict and change the availability of alcohol.

The nordic countries of Sweden, Norway, Finland and Iceland have implemented state-run monopolies on the sale of alcohol.

While the law isn't identical between these countries, the way these monopolies are run is very similar. In each case, the sale of most alcoholic beverages is restricted to only one state-owned chain of stores.

So are the monopolies working?

Alcohol consumption in APC, 1990-2017

In short, it's unclear.

Alcohol consumption in the nordic countries is in general lower than their neighbors, but not significantly so. Denmark for example has only slightly higher consumption without a history of alcohol monopoly.

It appears that there is more to alcohol consumption than where you can buy a beer. There are also significant cultural differences that can affect how and how much alcohol people consume in any given country that can tell a story beyond pure numbers.

A state run monopoly is better equipped to run educational campaigns and promote healthy drinking habits that can reduce behaviors like binge-drinking even when consumption is high. The exact impact of these monopolies is however controversial, and we encourage you to read more and form your own opinions.
